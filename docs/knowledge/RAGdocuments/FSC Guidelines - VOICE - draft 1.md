# FSC Guidelines - DG EMPL - VOICE - draft 1

## Introduction

### Purpose of These Guidelines

The purpose of these guidelines is to empower trade unions, European Works Councils (EWCs), Trade Union Alliances (TUAs), and workers' representatives with a clear understanding of the Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) certification and its connection to workers' rights. These guidelines aim to show how FSC certification can serve as a practical tool for promoting and protecting fundamental rights at work, strengthening trade union organizing, and supporting dialogue with multinational companies (MNCs) across global supply chains.

Trade unions can play a critical role in making FSC certification more robust and meaningful by ensuring that the social and labor standards embedded in the FSC Principles and Criteria are respected in practice. By engaging actively in FSC-certified workplaces and supply chains, unions can help guarantee that FSC's commitments to decent work, freedom of association, and collective bargaining are effectively implemented, while leveraging FSC certification to support their own organizing and bargaining strategies.

### Scope of These Guidelines

These guidelines focus on FSC Forest Management and Chain of Custody certifications, explaining their relevance to trade unionists and workers' representatives. They outline how FSC certification connects with international labor standards, including those set by the International Labour Organization (ILO), the United Nations (UN) Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, and the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises.

They also aim to bridge the gap between voluntary certification schemes and legally binding international regulations, such as the European Union's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD). By highlighting the practical uses of FSC certification in the context of these frameworks, these guidelines offer trade unionists concrete strategies to enhance their advocacy, organizing, and bargaining efforts in FSC-certified companies and supply chains.

FSC certification is not a substitute for strong union action but can be a valuable complementary tool for ensuring respect for workers' rights and promoting sustainable forest management. The active participation of trade unions in FSC processes—whether through direct engagement in audits, monitoring, and complaint mechanisms, or by using FSC standards in negotiations with employers—can help improve working conditions and defend workers' rights throughout global value chains.

### Why Is This Important for Workers?

In a globalized economy, decisions that affect workers are often made far away — in corporate headquarters, boardrooms, or investor meetings. These decisions shape working conditions, wages, safety, and rights, not just in factories or forests, but across entire supply chains.

For too long, companies have treated labor rights as optional — a matter of corporate image rather than obligation. Even when companies signed onto ethical sourcing policies or sustainability standards, enforcement was weak, and workers had little say in how those commitments were implemented.

Today, this is starting to change.

New legal frameworks — like the European Union's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD) and Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) — are forcing companies to take real responsibility for what happens in their global value chains. At the same time, certification systems like the **Forest Stewardship Council (FSC)** are being revised to better reflect workers' rights, particularly through **FSC Core Labour Requirements** and new obligations stemming from **Motions 50 and 51**, which explicitly protect freedom of association and occupational health and safety.

But none of this will matter unless workers and their unions are able to **use these tools strategically**.

That's what these guidelines are about:

- Demystifying FSC certification — what it is and what it isn't.
- Showing how it connects to workers' rights, international standards, and legal obligations.
- Offering practical strategies to **leverage FSC** to organize, bargain, report violations, and push for better conditions.
- Equipping trade unionists to combine FSC with broader global tools — including due diligence legislation, investor pressure, and company codes of conduct.

### About This Publication

These guidelines have been developed within the framework of the European Commission-funded project **VOICE**, aimed at strengthening trade union action in relation to global supply chain due diligence and sustainability standards. They are specifically designed for the affiliates of **UNI Graphical & Packaging (UNI G&P)** and the **Building and Wood Workers' International (BWI)**, providing practical tools to leverage FSC certification in organizing and advocacy strategies at both the company and sectoral levels.

# Chapter 1: Introduction to International Worker Rights Framework

## 1. Global Standards and Workers Rights

### Objective

This chapter introduces main global standards protecting workers' rights. It focuses on equipping trade unionists, workers' representatives, and European Works Councils (EWCs) with essential knowledge to leverage these standards in the context of FSC certification and broader organizing efforts.

### Scope

The main frameworks covered are:

- **ILO Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work**
- **UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs)**
- **OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises and Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct**

These instruments are complementary, and are to some extent embed in FSC certification's social standards. Both Global Standards and FSC offer strategic tools for unions engaging multinational companies (MNCs) across global value chains.

---

### 1.1 ILO Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work

#### What is the International Labour Organization (ILO)

The **International Labour Organization (ILO)** is a specialized agency of the **United Nations**, established in **1919**, dedicated to promoting **social justice** and internationally recognized **human and labor rights**. Its unique **tripartite structure** includes representatives from **governments**, **employers**, and **workers**, ensuring a balanced approach to labor standards and policies.

#### Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work

The ILO has identified **five core labor standards**, known as the **Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work**:

1. **Freedom of Association and the Effective Recognition of the Right to Collective Bargaining**: Workers have the right to form and join unions and to negotiate collectively with employers.
2. **Elimination of Forced or Compulsory Labor**: Prohibits all forms of forced or compulsory labor.
3. **Abolition of Child Labor**: Aims to eliminate child labor and ensure that children are not engaged in work that deprives them of their childhood or is harmful to their health and development.
4. **Elimination of Discrimination in Respect of Employment and Occupation**: Ensures equal opportunities and treatment for all workers, prohibiting discrimination based on race, color, sex, religion, political opinion, national extraction, or social origin.
5. **Occupational Safety and Health (OSH)**: Introduced in 2022, it recognizes the right to a safe and healthy working environment, emphasizing the prevention of accidents and injuries arising from work.

### Scope of Application

These principles are universally applicable to all **ILO member states**, regardless of whether they have ratified the specific conventions related to these rights. By virtue of their membership, countries commit to respecting, promoting, and realizing these principles in **good faith**. This commitment extends to **multinational companies** operating within these countries, providing a framework for unions to advocate for **workers' rights** across global supply chains.

### Grievance and Reporting Mechanisms

While the ILO does not have direct enforcement authority, it employs several mechanisms to monitor and promote compliance:

- **Supervisory System**: The ILO's supervisory system includes regular reporting by member states on the implementation of conventions they have ratified. These reports are reviewed by the **Committee of Experts on the Application of Conventions and Recommendations**, which provides observations and direct requests to governments.
- **Complaints Procedure**: Workers' organizations can file complaints against member states for not complying with ratified conventions. These complaints are examined by the **ILO's Governing Body**, which can appoint a **Commission of Inquiry** to investigate serious allegations.
- **Committee on Freedom of Association**: This committee specifically addresses complaints related to violations of the principles of freedom of association and the right to collective bargaining, even if the country concerned has not ratified the relevant conventions.

### 1.2 UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (UNGPs)

#### What are the UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights?

The UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights, endorsed by the United Nations in 2011, are a framework to ensure that businesses respect human rights across their operations. These principles are not legally binding but have become a widely accepted standard that many companies aim to follow.

The UNGPs are organized around three core pillars:

1. **Protect**: Governments have a duty to protect human rights, including labor rights, by implementing effective policies and regulations to prevent abuses by third parties, including businesses.
2. **Respect**: Businesses have a responsibility to respect human rights within their operations and supply chains. This involves avoiding infringing on the rights of others and addressing any adverse impacts on human rights with which they are involved.
3. **Remedy**: Both governments and companies must provide effective remedies when rights violations occur. For businesses, this includes setting up grievance mechanisms to address workers' complaints, offering a means for unions to advocate on behalf of affected workers.

#### Grievance Mechanisms within the UNGPs

The UNGPs emphasize that companies should establish **operational-level grievance mechanisms** accessible to all workers. These mechanisms provide a structured way for workers and unions to report concerns about rights violations and seek remediation. While not a replacement for judicial remedies, these grievance mechanisms offer a valuable avenue for addressing issues early and directly.

Grievance mechanisms can take many forms, including anonymous reporting channels, third-party mediation, or even partnerships with unions to facilitate issue resolution. Unions can leverage these channels to bring forward workers' concerns within FSC-certified companies, especially in cases of non-compliance with fundamental labor rights.

#### How Unions Can Use the UNGPs

Unions can utilize the UNGPs as a foundation for advocacy, particularly when engaging with FSC-certified companies that are committed to sustainable and responsible business practices. The principles provide a framework to:

- **Engage in Dialogue**: Use the "Respect" pillar to push companies to honor labor rights, particularly in regions where local laws are weaker.
- **Escalate Issues**: Grievance mechanisms under the "Remedy" pillar allow unions to address issues such as unfair treatment or unsafe working conditions, helping workers seek redress without resorting to legal battles.
- **Hold Companies Accountable**: By referencing the UNGPs, unions can demand that companies fulfill their responsibility to respect workers' rights as part of their commitment to ethical business.

#### Expanding Knowledge through Key Resources

For those interested in exploring the UN Guiding Principles further, the following resources are recommended:

- [UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights (Full Text)](https://www.ohchr.org/documents/publications/guidingprinciplesbusinesshr_en.pdf): The complete document outlining the principles and their applications.
- [UNGPs Implementation Guide](https://www.business-humanrights.org/en/un-guiding-principles): A practical guide to implementing the UNGPs, helpful for understanding specific applications within different business contexts.
- [OHCHR - Grievance Mechanisms for Business and Human Rights](https://www.ohchr.org/Documents/Publications/GuidingPrinciplesBusinessHR_EN.pdf): An overview of effective grievance mechanisms, explaining how they support human rights compliance in businesses.

### 1.3 OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises and Due Diligence Guidance

#### What are the OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises?

The OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises are a set of recommendations issued by the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) to promote responsible business practices. These guidelines cover a range of areas, including labor rights, environmental protection, and anti-corruption measures. While the guidelines are voluntary and not legally binding, they represent widely accepted standards that multinational companies, particularly those committed to sustainable practices, are encouraged to follow.

The guidelines include key protections for workers, such as:

- **Respect for Labor Rights**: Multinational companies are expected to uphold fundamental labor rights, including the right to collective bargaining, fair wages, and safe working conditions.
- **Non-Discrimination**: Companies should ensure equal opportunities and prevent discrimination in hiring, promotions, and workplace treatment.
- **Consultation with Workers**: Multinationals are encouraged to consult with workers and unions on changes that affect employment, such as restructuring or shifts in business practices.

These guidelines apply to all OECD member countries and to companies operating within or from these countries. As a result, they create an additional layer of accountability that unions can use to push for fair practices across supply chains.

#### What is Due Diligence and Why is it Important?

The **OECD Due Diligence Guidance** is a framework for companies to identify, prevent, and address risks related to human rights, labor standards, and environmental harm within their operations and supply chains. Due diligence involves:

1. **Identifying Risks**: Assessing areas where workers' rights or safety may be compromised, such as forced labor, unsafe conditions, or low wages.
2. **Preventing and Mitigating Risks**: Developing policies and actions to prevent violations or reduce harm, often involving partnerships with suppliers, unions, or local organizations.
3. **Monitoring and Reporting**: Regularly reviewing the effectiveness of due diligence actions and being transparent about outcomes.

For unions, due diligence requirements can be a valuable tool, especially within FSC-certified supply chains. These standards compel companies to take proactive steps to ensure they are not complicit in rights violations, allowing unions to demand transparency and accountability from employers.

#### Grievance Mechanisms within the OECD Guidelines

The OECD Guidelines include provisions for grievance mechanisms, known as **National Contact Points (NCPs)**, which allow workers, unions, and other stakeholders to raise complaints about violations of the guidelines. NCPs provide a mediation process, where companies and unions can come together to discuss alleged violations and seek resolutions.

Unions can leverage these grievance mechanisms to escalate issues of labor violations within multinational companies, particularly when these companies operate in countries with weaker labor protections. NCPs can be effective in drawing attention to systemic issues and pushing companies toward better practices.

#### How Unions Can Use the OECD Guidelines and Due Diligence Guidance

Unions can use the OECD Guidelines and Due Diligence Guidance to:

- **Push for Accountability**: Reference the guidelines to hold companies accountable for fair treatment of workers in their operations and supply chains.
- **Advocate for Worker Protections**: Leverage due diligence requirements to press for improved working conditions, safe environments, and fair wages.
- **Use Grievance Channels**: National Contact Points offer a structured pathway to report and mediate grievances, providing unions with an avenue for seeking redress when companies violate labor standards.

#### Expanding Knowledge through Key Resources

For additional information on the OECD Guidelines and Due Diligence Guidance, the following resources are recommended:

- [OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises (Full Text)](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-guidelines-for-multinational-enterprises-on-responsible-business-conduct_81f92357-en.html): The complete guidelines document, covering all areas of responsible business conduct.
- [OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Business Conduct](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/oecd-due-diligence-guidance-for-responsible-business-conduct_15f5f4b3-en.html): A detailed guide on implementing due diligence, with practical steps and examples.

## 2. Multinational Companies' Codes of Conduct

### Objective

This section aims to help workers' representatives and trade union officers understand what corporate Codes of Conduct are, how they relate to global standards like the ILO, UNGPs, and OECD Guidelines, and how unions can use them both as a tool for advocacy and as leverage in campaigns. It highlights the limitations of these codes, while also showing how, despite being voluntary and unilaterally written by companies, they can still be strategically useful.

### Scope

This chapter explains the nature of corporate codes of conduct, their legal status, and their practical role in corporate compliance and reputation management. It also outlines how workers can use these codes to identify contradictions between company promises and actual practices—especially when companies are FSC-certified or claim to uphold human rights and sustainability standards.

---

### What Are Codes of Conduct?

Codes of conduct are internal company policies that set out expected behaviors and principles that the company claims to follow, often including commitments to:

- Human rights
- Decent working conditions
- Environmental responsibility
- Anti-corruption
- Respect for freedom of association and collective bargaining

These documents are typically published on corporate websites and included in CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility) or sustainability reports. In most cases, they are written by the company itself, without input from workers or unions, and are not legally binding.

---

### Relationship with Global Standards

While most multinational companies base their codes on international frameworks like the ILO, UNGPs, or OECD Guidelines, they do so voluntarily. These codes often reference:

- ILO conventions (especially the core labor standards)
- The UN Global Compact
- The UN Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights
- The OECD Guidelines for Multinational Enterprises

This gives codes of conduct an appearance of credibility, but it's important to understand that referencing a standard is not the same as implementing it. Many companies adopt these codes mainly to satisfy investors, customers, or regulators—but may not put them into practice unless pressured.

---

### Why Does Their Non-Binding Character Matter?

Because these codes are not legally binding, companies are not legally accountable if they fail to comply with them. Enforcement often depends on **external pressure**—such as trade union campaigns, consumer actions, or investor concern—rather than legal mechanisms.

That said, failure to comply with their own codes of conduct can seriously damage a company's **reputation** and credibility. Companies that fail to "practice what they preach" risk losing:

- Consumer trust
- Investor confidence
- Access to sustainable markets (especially under frameworks like FSC certification)

This reputational risk gives unions and workers a **strategic opportunity** to raise grievances and demand action—both through formal channels and public campaigning.

---

### What Has Changed: The Impact of Sustainability Regulations

In recent years, there has been an **explosion in corporate sustainability reporting**—driven by new legal obligations, particularly in the EU. Under laws such as the **Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)** and the upcoming **Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)**, companies are required to:

- Disclose how they manage human rights and environmental risks
- Describe their due diligence processes
- Report on relevant **internal policies**, including **codes of conduct**

This means that while codes of conduct themselves are not legally enforceable, the **act of publishing and reporting on them has become mandatory** for many companies.

👉 **In practice**, this gives unions a new leverage point: **highlighting violations of a company's own code of conduct can also signal non-compliance with legally binding reporting duties** under CSRD or CSDDD. This turns reputational accountability into potential **regulatory exposure**.

---

### Why Do Codes Matter for Unions?

Even though they are non-binding, codes of conduct can be useful tools for workers and unions in several ways:

- **Expose contradictions**: If a company commits to respecting freedom of association in its code but engages in union-busting, unions can point to this contradiction to pressure the company—especially publicly or with investors.
- **Leverage FSC certification**: Companies certified by FSC are expected to meet social and labor standards. If they also publish a code of conduct referencing ILO or FSC principles, this provides multiple grounds for unions to question inconsistent behavior.
- **Use in grievance and dialogue processes**: Many FSC-certified companies or multinational firms include grievance mechanisms in their codes. Even if weak, these can serve as entry points for dialogue, negotiation, or campaigns.
- **Reputation and compliance risks**: Public exposure of violations of the company's own code—especially when tied to legal requirements under CSRD or CSDDD—can damage its image, scare investors, and lead to scrutiny from certifiers or regulators.

---

### Callout Box: What Workers Should Know

> ✅ **Understand the company's commitments**
>
> Familiarize yourself with the code of conduct. What does the company claim to support? Does it commit to freedom of association, health and safety, or non-discrimination? Compare these promises to reality.

> ✅ **Use the code as a starting point for dialogue**
>
> Raise violations of the code in meetings with management, in works council discussions, or in collective bargaining negotiations.

> ✅ **Link the code to binding standards**
>
> Point out when company commitments fall short of ILO conventions or EU regulations like CSRD or CSDDD. This strengthens your case.

> ✅ **Escalate when needed**
>
> Use grievance mechanisms if direct dialogue fails—this can include internal mechanisms, FSC complaint procedures, or even OECD National Contact Points.

---

### Conclusion

While codes of conduct are rarely enforceable by themselves, they represent a formal, public commitment by the company that unions can exploit. They are especially useful when:

- Combined with FSC certification or legal obligations (like CSRD or CSDDD)
- Used as evidence in campaigns, grievances, or audits
- Cited during consultation and collective bargaining processes

Workers' representatives should always **locate, analyze, and monitor** the company's code of conduct and sustainability report. Together, they provide powerful tools to **confront violations, demand compliance**, and strengthen organizing strategies.

## 3. International Regulations

### Objective

This section helps workers' representatives and trade union officers understand the evolving legal frameworks that require companies to respect human and labor rights across their operations and supply chains. It provides an overview of the key international and European **human rights due diligence (HRDD)** and **sustainability reporting** laws, and explains how unions can use these tools to demand transparency, prevent rights violations, and hold companies accountable — including in FSC-certified supply chains.

### Scope

This chapter focuses on four major legal instruments that impact workers' rights globally:

- The **EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)**
- The **EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)**
- The **French Duty of Vigilance Law**
- The **German Supply Chain Act (LkSG)** (note: becoming less relevant due to CSDDD superseding parts of it)

All of these laws **require companies to take active responsibility** for respecting human and labor rights — not just in their own operations, but also across subsidiaries, contractors, and suppliers, including in Global South contexts.

---

### Why This Matters for Workers

These regulations mark a **paradigm shift** in how workers' rights are protected and enforced.

Unlike most national labor laws — which only apply inside the country that passed them — these new laws create **binding legal obligations on companies that apply across their entire global value chain**. That means:

> 📌 A company headquartered in France, Germany, or the EU must respect workers' rights — not only at home, but **in every country where it operates or sources from**, if it wants to continue doing business in Europe.

For workers and unions, this opens new opportunities to challenge violations **based on foreign legislation**, even when local laws or enforcement are weak. It creates new leverage to:

- Demand respect for international labor standards
- Engage companies headquartered abroad
- File complaints that reach beyond national borders
- Link local struggles to global legal and reputational pressure

Workers' rights are no longer just a matter of voluntary corporate promises — companies are being **legally required** to:

- Map risks to labor rights
- Prevent violations in supply chains
- Address abuse when it occurs
- Disclose relevant information in public reports
- Provide remedy in case of harm

This opens up new **entry points for unions** to engage in formal dialogue, file complaints, and challenge abusive practices — especially where companies are also FSC-certified and claim to follow sustainability principles.

---

### Key Laws Explained

#### 🔹 Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD)

- Applies to large EU and non-EU companies operating in the EU market.
- Requires them to **identify, prevent, mitigate, and account for** human rights and environmental risks across their entire value chain.
- Includes the **right to freedom of association, collective bargaining, safe working conditions, fair wages, and non-discrimination** (aligned with ILO conventions).
- Companies must engage with stakeholders — this includes **trade unions**.
- Civil liability is foreseen: victims (including workers) can seek damages when harm results from company failure.

📌 *CSDDD replaces national frameworks like the German LkSG for companies it covers, setting a harmonized European standard.*

---

#### 🔹 Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)

- Expands the number of companies required to publish annual sustainability reports.
- Reports must follow **European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS)**, which include:
  - Respect for **fundamental labor rights**
  - Description of company policies (e.g. codes of conduct, grievance mechanisms)
  - Disclosure of **due diligence processes** and how stakeholders (including workers) are involved

📌 *This means companies can no longer hide behind vague CSR statements — they must provide detailed, audited information.*

---

#### 🔹 France – Duty of Vigilance Law

- In force since 2017.
- Applies to large French companies and their subsidiaries and suppliers worldwide.
- Requires the publication of a **"vigilance plan"**, including risk mapping and action plans.
- Allows **judicial action** by unions and NGOs in case of non-compliance.

📌 *The French law was a model for CSDDD. It remains relevant for companies not yet covered by EU directives.*

---

#### 🔹 Germany – Supply Chain Due Diligence Act (LkSG)

- In force since 2023.
- Requires large German companies to prevent human rights violations in their own operations and direct suppliers.
- Includes complaint mechanisms and the right to **lodge complaints via trade unions**.
- Now being partially superseded by CSDDD, but still relevant during the transition period.

---

### Conclusion

The emergence of HRDD and sustainability reporting laws gives unions a **legal framework** to hold companies accountable for workers' rights — not just morally, but **legally and financially**.

For unions active in FSC-certified value chains, these laws represent an opportunity to challenge companies's violations when combined with FSC's own social requirements. Together, they provide a **double mandate** — one from the certification scheme, and one from the law — to demand transparency, defend rights, and **build power for workers across global supply chains**.

## 4. FSC: What Is It and Why It Matters?

### Objective

This chapter explains how FSC Certification works and why it matters for workers. It focuses on how trade unionists and workers' representatives can use FSC's labour standards, audit mechanisms, and governance spaces to protect and advance workers' rights — especially freedom of association, collective bargaining, and health and safety — across global value chains in the graphical and packaging sectors.

---

### 4.1 What is FSC Certification?

![Image.tiff](https://resv2.craft.do/user/full/8e85d016-e339-fbd9-298c-a1281fb598fa/doc/A755EC11-E07A-48B2-8458-57C02EC5AD3D/B8577CA6-9B97-40D1-A484-EF8963461669_2/uyjAefShsexHgFc5ItDgH3ZenIUJ4kjj2hLUPIqtEXwz/Image.tiff)

#### Have you seen this logo?

You've probably noticed it printed on a cardboard box, a ream of paper, a tissue product, or even your company's sustainability report. If you see this logo at work, it means your company is **FSC-certified**. That isn't just a green label — it means your employer has made formal commitments, including to **respect your rights at work**.

If your company uses FSC-certified materials, it must follow FSC standards — and those include core labour rights such as:

- The right to **organize and bargain collectively**
- **No forced or child labour**
- **No discrimination**
- A **safe and healthy working environment**

---

#### What Is the FSC?

The **Forest Stewardship Council (FSC)** is a global non-profit organization created to promote **responsible forest management**. It does this by developing and enforcing standards that companies must meet if they want to use the FSC label on their products.

These standards go beyond forest protection — they include **social and labour rights**, based on international conventions. FSC's credibility comes from being a **multistakeholder system**, involving environmental NGOs, companies, and — increasingly — **workers and unions**.

FSC certification is **voluntary**, but once a company chooses to become certified, it **must comply** with FSC's rules — and undergo **independent audits** to keep its certificate.

> ⚠️ **However, FSC certification is far from perfect**. In many cases it is used primarily as a **market access tool** or for corporate image purposes, rather than reflecting a genuine commitment to workers' rights.
>
> We will examine these weaknesses, including issues with auditor independence and enforcement, in section **4.8 – Limits and Risks**.

---

#### Who Gets Certified?

There are two main categories of FSC certification:

1. **Forest Management Certification**: For companies that harvest or manage forests.
2. **Chain of Custody Certification**: For companies that process, transform, package, print, or sell FSC-labelled products.

These companies depend on FSC to sell their products to environmentally and socially conscious buyers — which makes FSC standards a **lever** for unions to push for stronger rights.

---

### What Does Certification Involve?

The process typically includes:

- An application by the company
- An audit by an independent third-party certifier
- Ongoing monitoring and regular re-certification
- Possibility of suspension if the company is non-compliant

Importantly, **trade unions and workers can provide evidence during audits** — a point we'll return to later in the chapter.

---

### 4.2 Why FSC Certification Matters to Multinational Companies?

FSC certification is not just a "green label" — it is a **strategic asset** for multinational companies, especially in packaging, printing, and tissue sectors. The main reasons they pursue it include:

1. **Access to Markets**
   - Many **retailers, brand owners, and government buyers** require FSC-certified products as a condition for doing business.
   - Large procurement programs (e.g., in the EU and North America) prefer or mandate FSC-labelled products.
2. **Customer Demand & Brand Reputation**
   - Consumers increasingly expect sustainability commitments.
   - The FSC logo is a **trusted and globally recognized mark** for responsible sourcing.
3. **Investor Confidence**
   - ESG-focused investors (**Environmental, Social, and Governance** — a common framework for evaluating a company's sustainability and ethical performance) look for credible third-party certifications.
   - FSC helps companies show they are managing environmental and social risks, including labour rights.
4. **Access to Finance**
   - Banks and lenders often consider sustainability certifications when assessing corporate risk profiles.
   - Certified companies may get **better loan terms** or qualify for green finance products.
5. **Regulatory Compliance & Risk Management**
   - Certification can help meet due diligence and sustainability disclosure requirements under laws like **CSRD** or **CSDDD**.
   - Helps companies show "evidence of compliance" in case of regulatory scrutiny.
6. **Competitive Advantage**
   - Differentiates a company from uncertified competitors.
   - Opens up contracts with large buyers that require FSC certification.
7. **Supply Chain Stability**
   - Certification helps companies secure reliable suppliers who meet both environmental and labour standards.

---

### 4.2 Why Does this Matters for Workers?

While FSC was created as an environmental certification system, labour rights entered the picture later, largely thanks to the **advocacy of its social chamber** — where trade unions, community groups, and social NGOs work to ensure that the human dimension of forestry and supply chains is not overshadowed by economic interests.

This implies that **certified companies commit to respect ILO's Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work,** as well as other rights and principles globally recognized. ### 4.2 FSC and Labour Standards: A Rights-Based Approach

FSC was created primarily as an environmental certification system. Labour rights entered the picture **later**, largely thanks to the **advocacy of its social chamber** — where trade unions, community groups, and social NGOs work to ensure that the human dimension of forestry and supply chains is not overshadowed by environmental or economic interests.

FSC has not developed its own labour rights standards from scratch. Instead, it has **incorporated references to the language of the ILO Core Labour Principles and Rights at Work** into its Principles and Criteria. While this is a significant step — because it makes those rights part of the certification's auditable requirements — it is **not the same as adopting the ILO Conventions in full** or ensuring enforcement equivalent to international labour law.

In other words:

> FSC certification reflects the *text* of key ILO principles, but its ability to guarantee those rights in practice depends on the quality of audits, the strength of complaints, and the active participation of workers and unions.

---

#### The ILO Core Labour Principles in FSC Standards

Since 2011, FSC's Principles and Criteria have included commitments based on the ILO Core Principles and Rights at Work. These cover:

| **ILO Convention** | **Right Protected**                  |
| ------------------ | ----------------------------------- |
| C087               | Freedom of association              |
| C098               | Right to collective bargaining      |
| C029               | No forced labour                    |
| C182               | Elimination of the worst forms of child labour |
| C111               | No discrimination at work           |
| C155 & C187        | Safe and healthy working environment |

For workers, this means that **FSC-certified companies have committed** to respect these rights, and violations can — in theory — lead to suspension or loss of certification. In practice, however, the enforcement of these commitments depends heavily on the **auditors' willingness to investigate, the robustness of the grievance process, and the pressure applied by stakeholders**.

> 📢 If your company is FSC-certified, it has committed to **respect your right to organize, negotiate, and work in safety**. This isn't just good practice — it's part of the rules they accepted.

---

#### From Principle to Practice

These labour-related commitments are concentrated in **Principle 2** (see section 4.3) of the FSC Principles and Criteria, which requires certified companies to maintain or improve the social and economic well-being of workers. Auditors are expected to verify, among other points:

- Whether workers are free to form and join trade unions of their choice
- Whether collective bargaining agreements are respected
- Whether working conditions meet safety and health standards
- Whether there is any evidence of forced, child, or discriminatory labour

While the inclusion of these points in an environmental certification is significant, **it does not automatically translate into strong protection**. The credibility of the process depends on:

- **How well audits are conducted**
- **Whether workers are interviewed independently**
- **How complaints are handled when violations are reported**

---

#### Strengthening the Labour Dimension: Motions 50 and 51

Two recent decisions from the FSC General Assembly — **Motion 50** and **Motion 51** — have the potential to strengthen the role of workers and trade unions within FSC:

- **Motion 50**: Establishes the fundamental **right of access** for trade unions and worker organizations to FSC-certified workplaces. Certified companies must allow union representatives to engage with workers at their first request without interference, and provide information on worker locations and schedules to enable this access.
- **Motion 51**: Guarantees workers' right to freely elect occupational health and safety (OHAS) representatives in all FSC-certified operations. Elections must occur at least every four years, with the number of representatives reflecting workforce size and workplace hazards.

These motions were won through **persistent advocacy from unions and social allies** within the FSC system. They are a **strategic breakthrough** for workers, and we will explore how to use them strategically in **section 5.6**.

---

### 4.3 FSC Principles and Criteria: What they say about workers?

The FSC **Principles and Criteria** (P&C) are the backbone of the certification system. Every FSC-certified company — whether it manages forests or operates in the chain of custody — must comply with these P&C to keep its certification.

While the P&C cover a broad range of environmental, social, and economic issues, **Principle 2** is the key one for workers. It requires certified companies to *maintain or improve the social and economic well-being of workers*.

---

#### Key Labour-Related Principles and Criteria

**Principle 2: Workers' Rights and Employment Conditions**

- **Criterion 2.1**: The organisation shall uphold the principles and rights at work as defined in the ILO Declaration on Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work (freedom of association, collective bargaining, elimination of forced labour, abolition of child labour, and elimination of discrimination).
- **Criterion 2.3**: The organisation shall provide a safe and healthy working environment, implementing measures to prevent accidents and injury, consistent with ILO Convention 155.
- **Criterion 2.4**: Wages, benefits, and working conditions shall meet or exceed legal requirements or collective bargaining agreements, whichever is higher.
- **Criterion 2.5**: Workers shall have access to mechanisms to raise grievances and resolve disputes, without retaliation.

**Principle 4: Community Relations**

- Includes clauses about engaging with local communities and Indigenous peoples, which can be relevant where workers live in affected areas.

---

#### Why Workers Should Know These Clauses

Quoting the **exact wording** of FSC's Principles and Criteria can be powerful in:

- **Audits**: Auditors must assess compliance with these criteria — presenting evidence in terms of a specific clause forces them to address it.
- **Negotiations**: If management ignores a union demand, pointing out that the same point is an FSC requirement can increase pressure.
- **Grievances**: FSC's dispute resolution process looks at whether companies comply with their Principles and Criteria; referencing them strengthens a case.

---

> 📢 **Tip:** In any FSC-certified workplace, request a copy of the company's certification scope and audit reports. Cross-check them against these Principles and Criteria to identify gaps between what's on paper and what happens in reality.

---

### 4.4 How FSC Audits Work and How Unions Can Engage

FSC certification is only credible if companies are regularly checked for compliance. This is done through audits carried out by accredited, independent certification bodies. In theory, these audits are meant to ensure companies meet all FSC Principles and Criteria — including the labour rights commitments we saw in 4.3.

In practice, the quality and depth of labour rights verification depends heavily on how the audit is done and whether workers and unions are actively involved.

#### Types of FSC Audits

1. **Initial Certification Audit**
   - First audit before a company is granted FSC certification
   - Most thorough — auditors review policies, practices, and compliance with all FSC Principles & Criteria
   - Union action: Prime opportunity to raise any known labour violations before the certificate is granted

2. **Annual Surveillance Audits**
   - Yearly follow-up to ensure continued compliance
   - More focused on specific areas, including issues raised in previous audits
   - Union action: Use any ongoing or new violations to push for corrective actions

3. **Full Re-Certification**
   - Conducted every 5 years
   - Full re-assessment of compliance, similar to the initial audit
   - Union action: Treat this as a second chance to challenge poor labour performance

4. **Unscheduled/Special Audits**
   - Can be triggered by serious complaints, major changes in operations, or high-profile allegations

#### Who Conducts FSC Audits?

Audits are performed by certification bodies accredited by Assurance Services International (ASI). While these bodies are supposed to be independent, they are paid by the company being audited, which can create conflicts of interest and inconsistent enforcement. This is why strong worker input is essential.

#### The Audit Process: Where Unions Can Act

**Pre-Audit Phase (4-8 weeks before audit)**

- Certification bodies announce upcoming audits and open stakeholder consultation periods
- FSC rules require active outreach to relevant stakeholders — unions qualify as such
- Union action: Submit written information on violations, request meetings with audit team

**On-Site Audit**

- Auditors visit facilities, review documents, and speak to management, workers, and stakeholders
- Worker interviews are mandatory where labour issues are relevant
- Union action: Ensure union reps and workers meet auditors confidentially without management present

**Post-Audit Phase**

- Certification body issues draft report to company, then final report
- Findings may include Non-Conformities (NCs) with deadlines for correction
- Union action: Request public summary, verify accuracy, follow up on corrections

#### Audit Methodology: Labour Rights Checks

When evaluating workers' rights, auditors are expected to:

- Review company policies and contracts
- Check compliance with national labour laws
- Interview workers about working conditions, freedom of association, and safety
- Verify existence of grievance mechanisms
- Inspect workplace for health and safety conditions

However, without union involvement, these checks can be superficial — especially if worker interviews are conducted with management present or without explaining the purpose.

#### Worker Interviews: Rights and Tips

Auditors must speak to workers directly and confidentially when evaluating labour criteria. Interviews can be individual or group, on-site or off-site.

Union tips:

- Prepare workers to describe facts clearly and truthfully
- Avoid scripted answers — focus on specific incidents and examples
- Emphasise patterns, not isolated events
- Ensure interviews happen without management presence

#### Stakeholder Consultation: Your Entry Point

Before every audit, certification bodies open stakeholder consultations. Unions can submit:

- Reports on violations
- Evidence of union-busting
- Copies of collective agreements or failed negotiation attempts

Submissions can influence audit scope — serious allegations require auditors to investigate.

#### How Findings Are Classified

**Major Non-Conformity (Major NC)**

- Serious breach of FSC standards
- Must be fixed within short time (usually 3 months) or certificate is suspended/withdrawn
- Union strategy: Major NCs are high-leverage moments

**Minor Non-Conformity (Minor NC)**

- Less serious but still requires corrective action, usually within 12 months

#### Consequences for Companies

- **Suspension**: Temporary loss of FSC certification until problems are corrected
- **Withdrawal**: Permanent loss of certification

For multinational companies, losing FSC certification threatens market access, investor confidence, and public image.

#### Union Tips for Maximising Impact

1. Track audit schedules — Ask certification bodies when audits are planned
2. Prepare evidence early — Don't wait for auditors to arrive
3. Coordinate with national and global unions — Multiple voices raise credibility
4. Follow up after audits — Demand public summaries and check corrective actions
5. Use audit findings in campaigns — Even minor NCs can be powerful organising tools

> **Key Point**: Auditors check compliance based on what they see and what they're told. If workers don't bring issues forward, those issues often remain invisible. For detailed step-by-step guidance, see the Audit Engagement Checklist in Annex 1.

---

> 📌 **Tip – FSC Audit Leverage**
>
> FSC audits are one of the few moments when *independent, external evaluators* come inside the company.
>
> If unions prepare well, these audits can:
>
> - Validate workers' testimonies
> - Force companies to address labour issues
> - Create formal records that can be used in bargaining, campaigns, or FSC complaints

---

> 📌 **Field Tip:**
>
> Keep your **evidence package** ready before the audit date. FSC audits are short — if you are not prepared, the opportunity to get worker issues on record may be lost for another year.

### 4.5 FSC Complaints and Dispute Resolution System

When a certified company violates FSC standards — including labour rights under Motion 50 — there is a formal system to hold it accountable: the **FSC Complaints and Dispute Resolution System**.

This system can be used to challenge:

- Violations of FSC's Principles and Criteria.
- Auditor negligence or bias.
- Certification bodies that fail to apply the rules properly.
- FSC's own decisions (in specific cases).

It is open to **workers, trade unions, NGOs, and other stakeholders** — meaning that unions and global federations can file complaints directly.

---

#### How the System Works

1. **Identify the Breach**
   - Link the violation directly to a specific FSC Principle, Criterion, or Motion 50 requirement.
   - Examples: denial of union access (Criterion 2.1 / Motion 50), refusal to bargain (Motion 50), failure to elect an OSH delegate (Motion 50).

2. **Gather Evidence**
   - Well-documented complaints have a much higher chance of success.
   - Include:
     - Written records (emails, meeting notes, letters).
     - Worker testimonies.
     - Photos, videos, or other proof.
     - References to national law, collective agreements, or ILO conventions.
   - Organise evidence clearly and in chronological order.

3. **Decide Where to File**
   - **First level**: Submit to the certification body that issued the certificate. They are required to investigate.
   - **If unsatisfied or for serious cases**: Escalate to FSC directly through its online complaint platform.
   - **If the problem is with the auditor or certification body**: File directly with **Assurance Services International (ASI)**, which accredits and monitors auditors.

4. **Follow Up**
   - FSC and ASI must give written responses within defined timelines, but delays are common — regular follow-up is important.
   - Keep a record of all correspondence.

---

#### Common Challenges and Weaknesses

- **Slow timelines**: Complaints can take months or years to resolve.
- **Company pushback**: Certified companies often try to dispute or delay complaints.
- **Variable quality**: Some certification bodies investigate thoroughly, others superficially.

This is why **coordinated pressure** is essential — using the FSC process alongside union campaigns, investor pressure, media work, and direct negotiations.

---

#### Union Strategy for Using the FSC Complaint System

1. **Never act alone**
   - Workplace reps should always involve their **national union** when preparing a complaint.
   - For maximum impact, coordinate with **global federations (UNI or BWI)** so the issue is raised simultaneously at multiple levels.

2. **Use complaints as part of a broader campaign**
   - A complaint is rarely enough on its own. Pair it with workplace organising, collective bargaining demands, and public exposure where appropriate.

3. **Push for serious classification**
   - Aim for the violation to be recognised as a **major non-conformity**, which forces the company to fix the problem quickly or risk losing certification.

4. **Track the process**
   - Keep checking on deadlines and responses.
   - Use delays or inaction to push for further escalation — for example, asking ASI or FSC to step in.

---

> 📢 **Tip – Coordinated Pressure Works Best:**
>
> A complaint filed from the workplace, backed by the national union, and reinforced by global federations, sends a clear message to FSC, auditors, and the company: *the problem will not go away quietly*.
>
> This multi-level approach increases the likelihood of corrective action — and in some cases can trigger broader improvements in corporate behaviour.

---

### 4.6 Limits and Risks: What FSC Is Not

FSC can be a valuable organising tool, but it is **not a guarantee** that a company fully respects workers' rights.

Understanding its limits helps unions and workers use it effectively — without placing unrealistic expectations on the system.

---

#### 1. Certification Does Not Mean Perfection

- FSC certification confirms that a company meets FSC standards **at the time of audit** — it does not mean violations never occur.
- Compliance can fluctuate between audits, especially if workers are not actively monitoring and reporting breaches.

---

#### 2. Auditor Limitations and Conflicts of Interest

- Certification bodies are **paid by the companies they audit**, creating a risk of bias or reluctance to issue non-conformities.
- Some auditors have limited training or experience with labour rights, focusing more on environmental criteria.
- Audit visits are typically announced in advance, giving management time to prepare a "clean" version of the workplace.

---

#### 3. Inconsistent Enforcement

- Different certification bodies may interpret FSC rules differently, leading to uneven enforcement.
- In some cases, serious labour violations have been documented by unions or NGOs, but certificates have remained active.

---

#### 4. Risk of Social Washing

- Companies sometimes use FSC certification mainly as a **marketing and market access tool** — not as a genuine commitment to social responsibility.
- This can lead to situations where the FSC logo is displayed prominently, while worker rights issues remain unresolved.

---

#### 5. FSC Relies on External Pressure

- In practice, many labour rights improvements under FSC happen **only when unions, workers, or civil society raise issues** and push for enforcement.
- Without organised pressure, violations may go unnoticed or uncorrected.

---

#### 6. Governance Imbalance

- While Motion 51 improves union participation in FSC governance, business and environmental interests still dominate decision-making.
- Labour issues can be sidelined unless unions actively engage in both workplace enforcement (Motion 50) and FSC governance processes.

---

> 📢 **Key Message:** FSC is a tool — not a solution in itself.
>
> Its effectiveness depends on **active worker participation**, strong documentation of violations, and coordinated union action at workplace, national, and international levels.

# 5. Using FSC in Union Strategies

## 5.1 How to Spot and Identify Violations

Many workplace problems can seem "normal" because they've been happening for years — but under FSC rules, they may be **serious violations**.

At the same time, some issues workers report are not covered by FSC at all.

Knowing the difference is key to using the system effectively.

---

### 1. What Counts as a Violation?

Under FSC's Principles, Criteria, and Motion 50, a violation is any **practice or policy** in a certified company that:

- Conflicts with FSC's **Core Labour Requirements** (drawn from the ILO Declaration on Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work).
- Breaks specific Motion 50 obligations, such as denying trade union access, refusing collective bargaining, or failing to elect OSH representatives.
- Breaches FSC's wider social requirements (e.g., discrimination, forced labour, child labour, unsafe work conditions).

---

### 2. Common Reasons Violations Go Unreported

- **Normalisation**: Workers see violations (e.g., management interference in organising) as "how things are" rather than as breaches.
- **Lack of knowledge**: Unions are unfamiliar with FSC standards and don't connect certain abuses (e.g., surveillance of union meetings) to FSC obligations or ILO Fundamental Principles and Rights at Work.
- **Fear of retaliation**: Workers hesitate to speak out, especially in non-unionised or hostile environments.

---

### 3. Common Mistakes in Reporting

- Reporting grievances that are real workplace problems but **not covered by FSC** (e.g., closures — which are a huge problem but not a violation of a principle — or minor disputes about shifts).
- Providing complaints without evidence, making them harder to investigate.
- Citing national law breaches without connecting them to FSC rules — weakening the FSC-specific case.

---

### 4. How to Link Violations to FSC Obligations

When spotting a possible violation, always ask:

1. **Which FSC Principle or Criterion does it break?**
2. **Does it violate one of the ILO core labour standards?**
3. **Is it connected to Motion 50 requirements?**
4. **Can I link it to a concrete, auditable obligation?**

The stronger the link, the harder it is for auditors or FSC to ignore.

---

### 5. Use the Annex for Guidance

We have prepared **Annex 1: Typical Violations – Examples by Principle/ILO Convention**, which includes:

- Practical examples of what each type of violation looks like in real workplaces.
- Indicators and red flags for each fundamental right.
- Tips on what evidence to collect for each type.

---

> 💡 **Strategic Tip – Court Cases and FSC Complaints**
>
> FSC will not review a complaint if the same case is already in court — they will wait for the court's decision before acting.
>
> At the same time, unions should not delay legal action solely in the hope that an FSC complaint will solve the problem. FSC complaints are slow, carry reputational risk for the company, but cannot be legally enforced.
>
> When considering an FSC complaint for a violation of fundamental principles and rights at work, keep these strategic points in mind:
>
> - Unions may prioritise legal action, since courts can issue enforceable rulings, and they do not want to delay legal accountability just to wait for an FSC review. This will not prevent the use of the FSC complaint procedure later — it will only make it slower.
> - In countries where courts are anti-union or company-oriented, unions may instead prioritise a public campaign in which the FSC complaint serves as a reputational risk for the company.
> - In all cases, FSC complaints should be planned as part of a **broader strategy**, never as a stand-alone solution.

---

## 5.2 Documenting and Acting on Violations

Once a violation is identified, the next step is to **document it properly**.

Under FSC rules, poorly documented cases are harder to pursue — but a strong, well-prepared file can be used both in local negotiations and in escalation to national or global levels.

This documentation will also be essential if a formal complaint is filed, either directly with FSC or through UNI G&P and BWI.

---

### 1. Core Documentation Requirements

Good documentation makes the difference between a weak allegation and a credible case that FSC auditors, management, or other stakeholders cannot ignore.

At the local level, this means:

**a. Establish the Union Rights Violation**

- Identify the specific ILO Convention violated (87, 98, 29, 105, 138, 182, 100, 111).
- Show a **systematic pattern**, not isolated incidents.
- Demonstrate the company's **direct or indirect involvement**.

**b. Evidence of Union-Busting or Rights Denial**

- **Recognition refusal**: Company refuses to recognise a legitimately formed union.
- **Collective bargaining obstruction**: Blocking or delaying negotiations.
- **Anti-union campaigns**: Intimidation, harassment, retaliation against organisers.
- **Legal manipulation**: Using "legal and judicial instruments" to block or delay recognition (as in the BILT case [https://connect.fsc.org/current-cases/policy-association-cases/ballarpur-industries-limited-bilt](https://connect.fsc.org/current-cases/policy-association-cases/ballarpur-industries-limited-bilt)).

**c. Documentation Chain**

- **Union formation records**: Date of creation, membership data, registration papers.
- **Formal requests**: Letters requesting recognition or bargaining, with proof of delivery.
- **Company responses**: Refusals, delays, legal challenges, counter-measures.
- **Impact on workers**: Lost wages, unsafe conditions, or other harms caused by lack of representation.

**d. Corporate Control Evidence**

- **Parent company involvement**: Show that the parent "exercised significant authority and control" over labour relations at its subsidiary.
- **Management directives**: Internal communications about union policy.
- **Consistent patterns**: Same anti-union practices in multiple sites or countries.

**e. Legal/Regulatory Context**

- National labour law requirements vs. company actions.
- Labour inspector reports.
- Court cases filed by the union.
- Any related ILO complaints or findings.

---

> 📌 **Tip – Normalisation of Violations**
>
> Many violations are **not reported** because workers and even local union reps consider them "normal" in their workplace or country.
>
> For example:
>
> - Management attending union meetings or "observing" recruitment drives
> - Persistent delays in bargaining that last years
> - Hazardous conditions treated as routine
>
> **If in doubt, document it** — a small issue locally may be seen as a serious breach when reviewed under FSC or ILO standards.

---

### 2. Evidence Standards

FSC requires **"clear and convincing evidence"** — meaning violations must be "substantially more probable to be true than not."

To meet this standard:

- Use **multiple sources**: worker testimonies, official reports, photographs, videos, internal documents.
- Ensure **timeline consistency**: events must follow a clear chronological pattern.
- Show the **systematic nature**: repeated violations, not one-off events.

---

### 3. Using Violations in Local Union Strategies

Once documented, violations can:

- Strengthen **collective bargaining** demands by showing the company is failing to meet its FSC obligations.
- Support **organising campaigns**, especially using Motion 50's right of access.
- Put **compliance pressure** on local management by highlighting the reputational risk of non-compliance.

---

### 4. Sharing and Escalating

- **Always coordinate with the national union**, as they can provide invaluable guidance and support throughout the documentation process. They are also available to step in whenever necessary..
- The evidence should be organised so it can be used immediately in local bargaining, public campaigning, or escalation to global federations.

> 📌 **Tip – Using the Template and Reporting to UNI G&P**
>
> You can use the **example template form** provided in annex 2 to structure your documentation in line with FSC requirements.
>
> If the case needs to be escalated beyond the local level, UNI affiliates can also report violations directly using the **UNI G&P online reporting form** (link in the annex 2).
>
> This ensures the case is ready for review by national unions, global federations, and, if needed, FSC itself.

---

> 📄 **Key Message:** The BILT case succeeded because BWI showed not only the rights violations, but also that the FSC-certified parent company was directly responsible for its subsidiary's anti-union actions. Local unions should aim to document both the violations **and** the corporate structure that makes the top company accountable.

## 5.3 Escalating to Global Federations (UNI G&P and BWI)

When a violation is **well-documented** but cannot be resolved locally, or when the case has **strategic relevance beyond one site**, it should be escalated to the **Global Union Federations (GUFs)** — UNI Graphical & Packaging (UNI G&P) and the Building and Wood Workers' International (BWI).

These federations can apply **pressure at the international level**, directly with multinational headquarters, FSC governance, investors, and other stakeholders.

---

### 1. When to Escalate

Escalation is not the first step, but it becomes necessary when:

- **Local negotiation fails** or management refuses to address the violation.
- The violation is **part of a broader corporate pattern** affecting multiple sites or countries.
- The case involves **serious breaches** of FSC obligations, especially of the ILO Core Conventions.
- There is **strategic value** in coordinated, cross-country pressure (e.g., a major organising drive or defending union presence in a key facility).

---

### 2. How GUFs Can Act

UNI G&P and BWI can:

- **Engage corporate headquarters**: Present the case directly to decision-makers with authority over all operations.
- **Coordinate cross-border solidarity**: Mobilise other unions in the company's network, including those in European Works Councils or Trade Union Alliances.
- **Use FSC governance channels**: Raise the case with the FSC International Secretariat, the Policy and Standards Committee, or during stakeholder consultations.
- **Leverage reputational pressure**: Engage with buyers, investors, NGOs, and media to highlight the risks of non-compliance.

---

### 3. Preparing for Escalation

Before sending a case to GUFs:

- Ensure the **documentation is complete and clear** (see 5.2).
- **Connect the violation to FSC obligations** and/or Motion 50 requirements.
- Include evidence of **local resolution attempts** (meeting notes, letters, refusal emails).
- If relevant, highlight **how the case links to broader corporate behaviour** in other countries.

---

### 4. The Escalation Path

1. **Local union** detects and documents the violation.
2. **National union** reviews and supports the case.
3. If unresolved or strategically significant → escalate to **UNI G&P and/or BWI**.
4. **GUFs, along with the affiliated unions**, coordinate the next steps, which may include:
   - Direct dialogue with the company's top management.
   - FSC-level interventions.
   - Coordinated campaigns across countries.
   - Use of legal or regulatory channels (e.g., OECD complaints, due diligence laws).

---

> 📌 **Tip – Strength in Numbers**
>
> Cases gain more weight when GUFs can show they represent **a network of unions** across the company's global operations.
>
> Even if the violation is local, share it — it may be part of a larger pattern the GUFs are tracking.

---

## 5.4 Role of EWCs in FSC and CSDDD Compliance

**European Works Councils (EWCs)** have a legal right to be informed and consulted by central management about issues that have a transnational impact on workers in Europe.

When a company is FSC-certified and/or covered by the EU Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD), these rights can be used to **reinforce compliance with workers' rights** across the company's global operations.

---

### 1. Why EWCs Matter in the FSC Context

- **Legal authority**: EWCs can require central management to disclose relevant information on risks, policies, and compliance measures — including those linked to FSC obligations.
- **CSDDD leverage**: The CSDDD obliges companies to identify, prevent, and mitigate human rights risks throughout their global value chains — and to discuss these risk assessments with EWCs. Full FSC compliance would help them meet these legal duties.

---

### 2. How EWCs Can Act

EWCs can:

- **Request documentation**: Ask for FSC-related audit results, corrective action plans, and grievance records, especially in high-risk countries.
- **Question due diligence reports**: Compare CSDDD-required risk assessments with FSC obligations and actual reports from local unions.
- **Bridge local–global information**: Receive reports from unions outside the EU and raise them with central management in EWC meetings.
- **Trigger follow-up actions**: Recommend management engage with local unions, take corrective measures, or cooperate with GUFs to resolve problems.

---

### 3. Coordination with Trade Union Alliances (TUAs) and GUFs

- **EWCs should not act in isolation**: Coordination with the Trade Union Alliance (if established) and the relevant GUF (UNI G&P or BWI) ensures consistency in demands and strengthens the pressure on management.
- This creates a **feedback loop**:
  1. Local unions document violations (5.2).
  2. EWCs receive and present them to central management.
  3. GUFs coordinate international follow-up and FSC-level interventions if needed.

---

### 4. FSC + CSDDD: Extending Compliance Beyond Europe

One of the most significant changes with CSDDD is that its obligations apply **globally**, not just to operations in EU countries.

This means:

- EWCs can ask about labour rights risks **outside Europe** and how the company's FSC obligations are applied in those locations.
- Violations in non-EU countries can still be part of the EWC–management dialogue if they pose a risk to corporate compliance under CSDDD.
- This creates an **enforcement bridge** between EU legal requirements and FSC certification, where EWCs become a vehicle to connect **real cases from the field** with the company's official risk management process.

---

> 📌 **Tip – EWCs as Compliance Gatekeepers**
>
> An informed EWC can ensure that violations detected by unions anywhere in the company's global operations are brought to the attention of central management, forcing them to address the issues before they damage both FSC standing and CSDDD compliance.
>
> This represents a **unique capacity for EWCs**, as they can connect the work of GUFs in identifying violations across the value chain with CSDDD compliance — improving company processes and reinforcing the enforceability of EU legislation **outside EU borders**, where EU legislators cannot directly reach.

## 5.5 Role of TUAs in Coordinating Global Pressure on Multinational Companies under FSC

**Trade Union Alliances (TUAs)** are UNI Global Union's primary structure for organising and coordinating union activity within specific multinational corporations (MNCs).

They bring together unions from all countries and regions where an MNC operates — ensuring that union strategies match the global scale of corporate decision-making.

---

### 1. Why TUAs Matter for FSC Enforcement

When a company is FSC-certified, any violation of FSC labour obligations (especially those related to ILO Core Conventions and Motion 50 rights) is not a local problem only — it is a global compliance risk.

TUAs can:

- **Consolidate reports** of violations from different sites or countries into a single, documented case.
- **Identify patterns** of anti-union behaviour or breaches of FSC standards across the corporate group.
- **Coordinate joint demands** to corporate headquarters, linking FSC compliance to wider corporate accountability.
- **Strengthen the reputational and commercial risk** for the company by showing that violations are systemic, not isolated.

---

### 2. How TUAs Operate

TUAs function as permanent, multi-regional platforms that:

- **Share information** about corporate behaviour across all operations.
- **Support affiliates** in bargaining and organising.
- **Coordinate strategic actions**, from letters and petitions to simultaneous global protests or media exposure.
- **Engage in dialogue** with multinational management where possible — but escalate to public campaigns if management refuses to address violations.

---

### 3. TUAs in the FSC Escalation Path

Within the FSC enforcement strategy described in these guidelines:

1. **Local union** detects and documents the violation (5.1 and 5.2).
2. **National union** supports and confirms the case.
3. If the case is unresolved or has broader relevance → escalate to the **GUF (UNI G&P or BWI)**.
4. The **GUF activates the TUA** to:
   - Circulate the case across all countries where the MNC operates.
   - Verify if similar violations are happening elsewhere.
   - Agree on a **coordinated action plan** combining FSC complaints, investor engagement, and public pressure.

This coordinated front increases the chance that management will act to protect its FSC certification, brand reputation, and market access.

---

### 4. Coordination with EWCs and GUFs

- TUAs work closely with **European Works Councils** when they exist, to make sure FSC-related violations are raised at the highest European management level and connected to **CSDDD compliance obligations**.
- GUFs (UNI G&P and BWI) provide **political leverage** with global corporate leadership, FSC governance, and external stakeholders, ensuring the TUA's campaigns have maximum reach.

---

### 5. Strategic Outcomes

TUAs aim to turn FSC enforcement into part of a **larger corporate accountability framework**, pushing for:

- **Concrete commitments** to respect workers' rights in all FSC-certified operations.
- **Corrective actions** in specific cases.
- **Global Framework Agreements (GFAs)** that bind the company to uphold international labour standards in all operations and supply chains — going beyond FSC requirements.

---

> 📌 **Tip – TUAs as FSC Multipliers**
>
> TUAs can transform a local FSC complaint into a **global pressure campaign**, showing the company and its stakeholders that workers are organised across borders, informed about FSC obligations, and ready to act in unison. This multiplies the reputational and compliance risk for the company, creating stronger incentives for change.

---

## 5.6 Leveraging Motions 50 and 51: Enforceable Rights for Union Access and Health & Safety

**Motion 50 and 51** of the FSC General Assembly 2022 represents one of the most important breakthroughs for trade unions within the FSC system.

When they are in force, they will apply to **all FSC certificate holders**, including both **Forest Management (FM)** and **Chain of Custody (CoC)** operations. They support the rights of workers to **organise freely**, to **elect safety representatives**, and to work in a **safe and healthy environment** — aligned with ILO Conventions C087, C155, and C187.

---

### 1. Motion 50 – Union Access as a Certification Right

**What it says:**

> *"Trade unions and worker organisations shall be granted access to workplaces to engage with workers. This right applies to all FSC certificate holders (FM and CoC). Union representatives must be given access at the first request and without interference. Certificate holders shall provide information on the location and schedule of workers to enable this access."*

**What it means for unions:**

- It is no longer up to management discretion whether unions can meet workers.
- This access is **a condition of certification**, not negotiable.
- Union access rights must be respected *without retaliation, discrimination, or obstruction*.

---

### ✅ How to Use Motion 50 Strategically

- **Know the wording**: Refer directly to the motion in letters or meetings when requesting access.
- **Document violations**: Record if access is delayed, denied, or obstructed. Note names, dates, and what was said.
- **Escalate violations**: Denials can be used in FSC complaints, audits, and certification reviews.
- **Involve your GUF**: Report access issues to UNI G&P or BWI. They can raise the case internationally or with FSC directly.

---

#### 📌 Field Tip – Be Proactive with Access

Use Motion 50 to:

| **📍 Situation**               | **🎯 Action**                                              |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| You're blocked from a CoC site | Cite Motion 50 and request access in writing              |
| You're denied a worker list    | Demand info on locations and shifts — it's part of the right |
| Access is delayed              | Document and prepare to file a complaint                  |

Motion 50 isn't theoretical — it's operational. Use it.

---

### 2. Motion 51 – Elected Safety Representatives for All Workers

**What it says:**

> *"All workers in FSC-certified operations shall have the right to freely and frequently elect occupational health and safety (OHAS) representatives. Elections must occur at least every four years. The number of representatives must reflect the number of workers and workplace hazards. Certificate holders must support elections and ensure OHAS training and representation."*

**What it means for unions:**

- OHS representation is now a *standardised, enforceable right* under FSC.
- Applies equally in FM and CoC — from forests to packaging plants.
- Aligns FSC with **ILO's updated fundamental principles** (C155 + C187).

---

### ✅ How to Use Motion 51 Strategically

- **Demand elections** where they haven't happened — certificate holders must comply.
- **Request training** and capacity-building for elected representatives.
- **Check ratios**: Is the number of reps proportionate to workers and risks?
- **Use in bargaining**: Push for recognition of OSH reps and their rights.

---

#### 📌 Field Tip – OHAS as an Organising Entry Point

| **🛠 Workplace Situation**   | **💥 Strategic Use of Motion 51**                 |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| No elected safety rep exists | Demand elections citing Motion 51                 |
| Elected reps not trained     | Request training as per certification requirement |
| Management undermines reps   | Escalate through union + FSC complaint            |

Motion 51 gives health and safety a **worker-driven structure**, backed by certification pressure.

---

### 3. Coordinating Across Levels – Local to Global

To get the most out of Motions 50 and 51:

| **🔹 Level**                     | **🔧 What to Do**                                       |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Local Unions**                 | Request access, demand elections, monitor violations    |
| **National Federations**         | Support escalation, train reps, link to CB or litigation |
| **EWCs / TUAs**                  | Use FSC leverage during info/consultation processes     |
| **Global Federations (UNI/BWI)** | Raise cases globally, file complaints, build pressure   |

---

### 💡 Key Takeaway for Worker Representatives

> 🛠 **Motion 50 gives you access. Motion 51 gives you safety. Both give you leverage.**
>
> But leverage only works if you use it.
>
> - Learn the rules.
> - Document violations.
> - Engage your union.
> - Escalate strategically.
> - Combine FSC pressure with collective power.

---

> 📌 **Tip – Always Involve Your Union and GUF**
>
> Whenever Motion 50 or 51 rights are denied, **work with your national union and your GUF (UNI G&P or BWI)**.
>
> A coordinated approach — with local evidence and global leverage — makes it harder for the company to ignore the violation and increases the reputational and compliance risks to their FSC certification.

---

## 5.7 From FSC Leverage to Global Framework Agreements

**Global Framework Agreements (GFAs)** are negotiated between a multinational corporation and one or more Global Union Federations (GUFs) to set common labour standards for all company operations worldwide.

They are aimed at **levelling the playing field for organising** and giving unions the most direct channel to monitor and address compliance with fundamental principles — with an **extended capacity to intervene directly with company management** when issues arise.

---

### 1. Why GFAs Matter in the FSC Context

Using FSC leverage can be a stepping stone toward negotiating a GFA.

- **FSC as an entry point** – When violations are documented and challenged through the FSC system, it creates pressure on the company to address them consistently.
- **Evidence for global negotiations** – Well-documented FSC-related violations show patterns of behaviour across countries, giving GUFs and TUAs a stronger case for establishing a global agreement.
- **Extension of social dialogue** – GFAs enhance and formalise ongoing social dialogue within multinational companies, creating channels that go beyond national limitations.

---

### 2. How FSC Campaigns Can Lead to GFAs

1. **Local documentation and action** – Violations are identified and challenged through Motion 50, audits, or FSC grievance procedures.
2. **International coordination** – TUAs and GUFs consolidate evidence from multiple sites and countries, presenting a global pattern of rights breaches.
3. **Corporate engagement** – FSC compliance discussions can open the door to broader commitments.
4. **Global dialogue as reliability** – The global trade union movement can be a more reliable partner for upholding workers' rights and maintaining FSC credibility if companies engage in **permanent, structured dialogue** with GUFs.
5. **Negotiating the GFA** – FSC-related commitments can be built into a GFA, making them part of a broader, ongoing framework for monitoring and improvement.

---

### 3. Benefits of GFAs

- **Social dialogue and accountability** – GFAs create a structured platform for unions and management to address problems proactively, rather than reactively.
- **Direct union involvement** – They guarantee a recognised channel for dialogue and monitoring between management and unions at all levels.
- **Supply chain reach** – Many GFAs include clauses extending protections to suppliers and contractors.

---

> 📌 **Key Message**
>
> FSC leverage can be the **first pressure point** in a longer strategy. By connecting local violations to a global pattern, TUAs and GUFs can push companies to establish GFAs — creating structured, ongoing dialogue to monitor and enforce respect for fundamental workers' rights across all operations.
